Economic Research & Data Analysis

One of my primary passions is economic research and data analysis. In today's data-driven world, the ability to extract meaningful insights from large datasets has become increasingly valuable. I've spent considerable time learning statistical programming languages like R and Python, which have become essential tools in modern economic analysis. Through my coursework and independent projects, I've worked on analyzing various economic datasets, from unemployment trends to consumer spending patterns, using econometric techniques to identify relationships and test hypotheses.

What excites me most about economic research is the detective work involvedâ€”formulating questions, gathering evidence, and piecing together narratives that help explain economic phenomena. I recently completed a research project examining the impact of minimum wage increases on small business employment in Massachusetts, which required collecting data from multiple sources, cleaning and organizing it, and running regression analyses. The process taught me not only technical skills but also the importance of careful methodology and clear communication of results.

Beyond the technical aspects, I'm deeply interested in behavioral economicsâ€”the study of psychological, cognitive, and emotional factors that influence economic decisions. The work of economists like Daniel Kahneman and Richard Thaler has shown that people don't always behave as the rational actors assumed in traditional economic models. Understanding these behavioral insights is crucial for designing better policies and business strategies. I'm particularly fascinated by how nudges and choice architecture can be used to help people make better financial decisions without restricting their freedom of choice.
